Introduction

I've been following the current Ebola outbreak in West Africa since the first reports of it in March. Ebola has held a harrowing place in my heart since I read The Hot Zone, by Richard Preston, years ago. Any time I hear of an outbreak, I pay close attention, because of Ebola's potential to be horribly destructive on a global scale.

So far - even with the current record-breaking outbreak - that potential has thankfully gone wholly unfulfilled. Ebola is usually so deadly that it "burns itself out" before it has a chance to really wreak havoc. Person "A" gets it, maybe transmits it to person "B", and then they're both dead within a day or two and it doesn't spread any further. Morbidly, Ebola's biggest weakness has been its terrifying deadliness.

This outbreak is different, though; some 13,000 people have caught it, and 6,000 of those have died. Each individual who gets Ebola in Africa, during this outbreak, spreads it to between 1.51 and 2.53 others, on average. What do these numbers mean? I put together a very simplified model of those values. Let's just say that I wish my stock portfolio would grow at these rates.

There is a reason the World Health Organization is predicting 10,000 new infections per week in December, and some headlines are predicting hundreds of thousands, possibly millions infected in the new year. The outbreak in Africa is right around the bend in this curve between week 20 and week 25. Without effective intervention, things could get really bad, indeed.

But why is this outbreak different? There are a number of reasons, and some of them include lessons for us to consider as we face the prospect of fighting Ebola on our own shores:

  • The most painfully affected countries have recently gone through periods of civil war, so their healthcare systems are in shambles.
  • There is a local custom in West Africa of bathing the corpse of loved ones. This is a group affair, and involves hugging the corpse, washing it, etc., without any protective gear. This practice is thought to have contributed greatly to Ebola's devastating effects in West Africa.
  • In West Africa, superstition trumps science for many people. As a result, during this outbreak, there have been a number of incidents where mobs would "rescue" Ebola patients, would threaten, attack, or outright murder healthcare workers, or spread rumors that Ebola is a hoax.

How accurate is the "Party Line?"

Unless you've been totally ignoring the news for the past few months, you've probably heard the standard statements about Ebola:

  1. Ebola has an incubation period of up to 21 days
  2. Ebola is only transmitted through direct contact with bodily fluids from an infectious person.
  3. Somebody who is infected with Ebola, but is not showing symptoms, is NOT contagious.
  4. Ebola is up to 90% fatal
  5. Ebola is not airborne

I wondered: what does the science really say about these statements? How accurate are they? Is there evidence to suggest any of them is untrue? After all, if this web comic says that scientific reporting is suspect, maybe that's the case for Ebola reporting, too. So I dug in a little bit. Here's what I found.

  1. Ebola has an incubation period of up to 21 days.

    Well... maybe.

    ... for outbreaks of Ebola Zaire HF they have varied from 3-12 days. Overall, these reports indicate a range of 3-13 days for the incubation period. [Kortepeter, et al.]

    The source here is a summary of Ebola cases as of 2011, which was kind enough to include references to the source material. Where this material was not pay-walled, I followed the links and read the original reports. Unfortunately, the information for incubation period is a little bit sketchy, though for good reason; you can't go inoculate people with Ebola to observe the incubation period. Therefore, to determine what the incubation period is, you must interview patients to figure out when their exposure was, and hope the interviews are accurate. In one of the papers cited by Kortepeter et al, it appears that less than 5% of cases could be investigated with enough precision to determine when their exposure was:

    The incubation period could be determined with precision for only 5 patients, all of whom were health care workers (2 died and 3 survived). These 5 patients had had direct contact with patients (no needlestick incidents) during a maximum of 1 day; they became ill a mean of 6.2 days (range, 5 - 8) after the contact. [Bwaka et al]

    The Bwaka paper describes an outbreak in 1995 often referred to as the Kikwit outbreak, because it took place in Kikwit, Democratic Republic of the Congo. The paper covers 103 patients, yet only 5 patients could have their incubation periods reliably determined. Given the uncertainty associated with these numbers, as well as the lethality of the disease, tripling the roughly one week observed period seems a prudent safety factor.

    But there's at least one paper that suggests even 21 days is not conservative enough - there was at least one case of a (suspected) 25 day incubation period:

    The maximum incubation period, defined as the median duration of the interval from the onset of fever in the primary case to the onset of fever in the first secondary case, was 17 days (range, 9 - 25). [Dowel et al]
  2. Ebola is only transmitted through direct contact with bodily fluids from an infectious person.

    One of the things that has disturbed me during this outbreak is the conflicting information we're getting from news sources. On the one hand, politicians are quick to remind us that you have to have close, direct, contact with an infectious person. On the other, we have articles like this from Scientific American, which weave tales like this:

    Ambulances are scarce in areas of west Africa roiled by the Ebola outbreak, so when ill patients and their families need to go to the clinic they often turn to the next best thing‐a motorcycle taxi. An ill passenger will wrap her arms around the driver's waist and away they will go‐sometimes driving an hour or so to get to a nearby clinic. During the journey a weak patient, clinging to the driver, may expel diarrhea and literally drape herself over the driver even as her bodily fluids permeate the seat. In the process the driver may get infected.

    I don't know about you, but I wouldn't want to be the next person on that motorcycle taxi, regardless of whether Ebola is transmissible that way. The CDC's take is this:

    Ebola on dry surfaces, such as doorknobs and countertops, can survive for several hours; however, virus in body fluids (such as blood) can survive up to several days at room temperature. Q&As on Transmission

    Unfortunately, that page on the CDC's website doesn't maintain links to source studies.

    So what do actual researchers say? Well, trying to find papers on that sort of depends on figuring out the requisite clinical terminology. If you try, as I did, searching for "ebola out of body," you'll come up with a bunch of hokum about out-of-body experiences. The correct term to use is "fomite," which is defined as any object which can transfer germs from one host to another. So a contaminated needle is a fomite, as would be a contaminated motorcycle seat.

    When you use Google Scholar to search for "Ebola transmission fomite," you can find about 1100 articles. Obviously I didn't go through all of them - (I do have a day job after all...) but here are some of the interesting studies I did find.

    • Bausch, et al - Tested surfaces in a hospital isolation-ward setting, where floors were sprayed with bleach each morning, and other surfaces on an "as-needed" basis. The good news here is that this found that "environmental contamination and fomites do not appear to pose a significant risk when currently recommended infection control guidelines for the viral hemorrhagic fevers are followed." This seems to contradict the experience we've seen in West Africa and in Dallas, where healthcare workers have taken the brunt of this illness particularly hard. In Dallas, though, the nurses were apparently sent in without significant training on those guidelines, and in West Africa, there was no real healthcare system to speak of, and video of their isolation wards suggests they've had a difficult time implementing those guidelines, too.
    • Dowell, et al - A field study where they checked in Kikwit to see how close contacts (family members living with a patient) fared, and worked backwards from this to identify risk factors. Among the patients in the study, 95% died. This study offers the strongest evidence I've found that the claim is true that direct physical contact is required to transmit Ebola.
      Of 95 family members who had such contact, 28 became infected, whereas none of 78 family members who did not touch an infected person during the period of clinical illness were infected.
      One paragraph from this study should be comforting to parents.
      Children were relatively spared in this outbreak [Kikwit], as has been typical for the previous EBO outbreaks and for certain other viral hemorrhagic fevers. Only 27 (9%) of the 315 persons in Kikwit who developed EHF were <17 years of age, and in this study, adult household contacts were at considerably increased risk compared with children. Much of this risk was related to more frequent exposure of the adults to ill family members and their body fluids, but elevated risk for adults remained even after adjusting for their increased exposures. Thus it remains possible that in addition to being less likely to be exposed to EBO, children are less susceptible to infection or severe disease. This question requires further study.
    • Francesconi, et al. Another field study looking at the Uganda outbreak from August 2000 through January 2001. This is the only paper I was able to find which discussed correlations between specific modes of fomite transmission.
      Regarding indirect transmission, sleeping on the same mat (PPR = 2.78, 95% CI 1.15 to 6.70), participating in the ritual handwashing during the funeral ceremony (PPR = 2.25, 95% CI 1.08 to 4.72), and sharing a communal meal during the funeral ceremony (PPR = 2.84, 95% CI 1.35 to 5.98) were significantly associated with disease. Although the differences were not statistically significant, sharing meals, washing clothes, and sleeping in the same hut were associated with a higher risk of acquiring the disease.
  3. Somebody who is infected with Ebola, but is not showing symptoms, is NOT contagious.

    This is perhaps the most troubling assertion I see in the mainstream, for several reasons:

    • None of the papers I read talked about the basis of this claim - and instead simply report it as gospel.
    • Other diseases are reportedly contagious during the incubation period. (The Flu is contagious from 1 day before symptoms appear to 5-7 days after - longer for children according to the CDC )
    • Several of the papers I read discussed patients who had gotten sick with no known exposure. It seems extremely unlikely that Ebola would spring into existence inside an individual without an actual exposure, and much more likely that although we do not know when or how those patients were exposed, an exposure did happen. Was this exposure to an asymptomatic patient? By definition, we don't know.
    • The belief that Ebola is not spread during the incubation period seems to rest on the idea that since Ebola is spread via bodily fluids, it can only be spread once the virus spreads from the point of initial infection into the patient's blood stream (this is called viremia). However, multiple papers refer to the practice of sharing meals, and specifically the practice of eating finger-lickin' food from a communal plate, as being high risk. Presumably these are high risk because virus from an index patient's mouth is transferred to the food on the plate, and then to other people. In other words, there seems to be evidence that saliva is one of the bodily fluids which communicates Ebola. I found no studies testing Ebola in saliva in the period before viremia.
    • Studies [1] [2] [3] show that in various outbreaks, people became infected with Ebola, but never showed symptoms. The presence of Ebola in these people was determined by blood tests. So how do we know that such fortunate people cannot transmit Ebola, like Typhoid Mary? Again, I found no studies discussing this question by way of numbers.

      The most reassuring discussion of this question in any of the papers was this frankly-written paragraph:

      If symptomless Ebola carriage posed a significant risk of infection, evidence of sporadic cases of haemorrhagic fever associated with, but not directly linked to, major outbreaks of Ebola infection should be identifiable by standard epidemiological techniques. Unfortunately, social and political conditions in central Africa seriously impede contact tracing. However, within these limitations, transmission of Ebola has been observed to occur only after close personal contact with affected patients or by exposure to infected body fluids or organs. Every outbreak of Ebola to date has been contained by the institution of standard infection-control measures: effective body disposal, barrier nursing, destruction or sterilisation of contaminated equipment, and the appropriate use of gloves, masks, and gowns.7 This degree of containment would be virtually impossible if symptom-free carriers posed a significant threat of infection. Baxter

    • On the other hand, Dr Bruce Beutler, the nobel-prize winning director of the Center for the Genetics of Host Defense at UT Southwestern Medical Center, had this to say on the topic
      “It may not be absolutely true that those without symptoms can’t transmit the disease, because we don’t have the numbers to back that up,” said Beutler, “It could be people develop significant viremia [where viruses enter the bloodstream and gain access to the rest of the body], and become able to transmit the disease before they have a fever, even. People may have said that without symptoms you can’t transmit Ebola. I’m not sure about that being 100 percent true. There’s a lot of variation with viruses.” nj.com
      And if you think about it, this makes sense. Testing for Ebola in the blood stream is expensive, so nobody goes around testing people unless they already have symptoms. So we don't know what their blood looked like the day before they became symptomatic.

    Overall, I side with Dr Beutler, and do not trust this assertion.

  4. Ebola is up to 90% fatal

    Well, that's pretty scary, isn't it? Notice that "up to" bit, though? It actually should say up to 100% fatal, because in a 2011 outbreak, precisely one person got sick, and that person died.

    It appears, especially given the level of success being experienced at US hospitals, that the quality of care given to a patient can have a dramatic effect on the outcome. So far, 1 person has died in the US of Ebola, while 7 people have been treated here. That works out to only a 14% fatality rate. Those still aren't odds I'd want to take, but it's not quite as terrifying as the 90% number that usually ends up in headlines.

    So what are the actual numbers? Fortunately, the CDC has a convenient table we can look at which outlines all known Ebola outbreaks here .

    I copied the CDC data to a Google Spreadsheet so that I could toy around with the numbers some. What I find is that the average lethality is around 57%. I was also able to put together some charts that I find interesting.

    This chart compares the number of cases in a given outbreak with the lethality during that outbreak. I don't see a particularly strong correlation between the two, though I do find it interesting that the two most broadly-spread outbreaks had fatality rates closer to 50% than to 100%.

    This chart shows fatality rate over time. Again, there's no correlation, but it's interesting to zoom around and see the magnitudes involved in various outbreaks. Sorry - this one requires flash, so it may not work for folks on iOS or who haven't updated flash in a while.

    This chart compares Ebola subtypes with their respective fatality rates.

  5. Ebola is not airborne

    That's a reassuring thing to hear. I don't know about you, but if something is 57% fatal (or, as the headlines spew, UP TO 90% FATAL!!!), I don't want to risk getting it just by walking outside. If something that fatal were airborne in the way the flu is ‐ or, God forbid, a strain of flu emerged which was that deadly ‐ it would be time to consider making a fashion statement by wearing a HEPA-filtered hood at all times.

    But is it really not airborne? What's the science say?

    • Baron, et al
      The absence of illness among persons who were exposed to cases in confined spaces, but without physical contact, confirmed previous impressions that there is no risk of airborne transmission.
    • Martin-Morena, et al
      Ebola is rarely transmitted via an airborne route.
      This paper goes on to discuss that because it is rare for Ebola to be transmitted by an airborne route, respiratory protection is unnecessary, and should not be used, because
      the image of workers with spectacular protective clothing might contribute to the panic in some communities. If this leads people to flee affected areas it could increase the spread of infection. It also reinforces the view that some lives are more valuable than others, already engendered by decisions about the use of experimental Ebola drug ZMapp.
      I would argue that this is an argument that we should innovate to produce less expensive resperatory protection gear so that it becomes commonplace, and that we should educate so that people understand it is precautionary and meant to ensure not only that the healthcare worker doesn't get sick, but that the worker doesn't spread the disease, either.
    • Johnson, et al. This experiment involved aersolizing ebola virus and exposing 4 rhesus monkeys to the resulting aerosol, while refraining from giving exposure to 2 control monkeys.
      The potential of aerogenic infection by Ebola virus was established by using a head-only exposure aerosol system. … Clinical disease occurred in all four Ebola virus exposed monkeys, but not in either of the control monkeys.
      In other words, you can probably get Ebola by inhaling it, because monkeys definitely can. This seems to contradict the prior two papers. I think this can be reconciled by hypothesizing that Ebola virus does not tend to shed through the lungs and therefore does not get aerosolized in nature. So even though the nose, mouth and lungs are capable of being airborne infection sites, they do not appear to produce virus in an aerosolized manner.
    • Weingartl, et al. This experiment involved innoculating six pigs, and subsequently putting them in cages in the vicinity of, but without direct contact to, six monkeys. The six monkeys got sick:
      Here we show ZEBOV transmission from pigs to cynomolgus macaques without direct contact. Interestingly, transmission between macaques in similar housing conditions was never observed. Piglets inoculated oro-nasally with ZEBOV were transferred to the room housing macaques in an open inaccessible cage system. All macaques became infected. Infectious virus was detected in oro-nasal swabs of piglets, and in blood, swabs, and tissues of macaques. This is the first report of experimental interspecies virus transmission, with the macaques also used as a human surrogate.
      … Under conditions of the current study, transmission of ZEBOV could have occurred either by inhalation (of aerosol or larger droplets), and/or droplet inoculation of eyes and mucosal surfaces and/or by fomites due to droplets generated during the cleaning of the room. Infection of all four macaques in an environment, preventing direct contact between the two species and between the macaques themselves, supports the concept of airborne transmission. It is of interest, that the first macaques to become infected were housed in cages located directly within the main airflow to the air exhaust system. The experimental setting of the present study could not quantify the relative contribution of aerosol, small and large droplets in the air, and droplets landing inside the NHP cages (fomites) to EBOV transmission between pigs and macaques. These parameters will need to be investigated using an experimental approach specifically designed to address this question.

    In summary, I think it is possible to transmit Ebola via an airborne route (by cough or sneeze droplets), I agree that it is not transmitted from humans in an aerosolized manner. If it were, I think it would be much more contagious and we would see much faster and deadlier spread.

    Because I believe it is possible to transmit Ebola via airborne routes, I wholeheartedly disagree with Martin-Morena, and think healthcare workers should absolutely wear respiratory protection.

Conclusions

Here's how I rank my confidence in the 5 statements that I set out to investigate:

  1. 80% - Ebola has an incubation period of up to 21 days There have been cases where it might have been as much as 25 days. It seems like it's usually only around 6 or 7 days though, so the odds are that 21 is still on the conservative side
  2. 90% - Ebola is only transmitted through direct contact with bodily fluids from an infectious person. As long as we remember that those bodily fluids don't necessarily have to be in or on the infectious person, I believe this statement is true. It doesn't appear that humans aerosolize Ebola, but pigs might.
  3. 40% - Somebody who is infected with Ebola, but is not showing symptoms, is NOT contagious. I worry quite a bit about this because there are no numbers I could find to back it up.
  4. 0% - Ebola is up to 90% fatal I believe it's actually 57% fatal, with wide variation largely due to quality of care, on an outbreak-to-outbreak basis.
  5. 90% - Ebola is not airborne I'm not worried about walking outside, but I wouldn't want to be in the same room as an Ebola patient without respiratory protection

I'm not a doctor; all I've done is reviewed a bunch of scientific papers and drawn my own conclusions. I'm not trying to instill fear; if anything, I hope that by helping readers to get a deeper understanding of the science, they can draw their own conclusions. The papers are out there; you don't have to take my word for it, and you don't have to take politicians' or pundits' words for it, either.
